# 07 · What the tool cannot see

Chapter 06 named what the tool *can do, but must do carefully*.

This last chapter names what the tool *cannot reach at all*. The list is not a rhetorical modesty — it is a statement of principle.

---

## One · The knowing of the body

Whether today's breath is shallow or full, whether something is held in the chest, whether the spine is leaning off to one side when the user sits — the machine does not know.

Embodied knowing does not translate into text. When the user later writes *"my chest felt tight"*, the machine reads the phrase, not the tightness.

---

## Two · This very present moment

Records are, by definition, past. Presence is now.

How much of the mind is actually in the room at the time of reading — how much is here and how much is scattered — is in no data set.

Every observation the system offers is about some **earlier** version of the reader, not the one reading this sentence.

---

## Three · The quality of silence

The heart of contemplative practice tends to happen where language ends. A system that can only operate in language is structurally barred from the place where the real work is done.

*"It was quiet today"* arrives to the machine as a string of words, not as the depth that the quietness had.

---

## Four · What watching itself is like

When a practitioner is watching — an emotion, a thought, a pattern as it arises — what that watching feels like, from the inside, is not recoverable from the report afterward.

The machine can say *"you've repeated this pattern eleven times"*, but the act of **seeing through** the pattern in a given moment is available only from that moment.

---

## Five · What passes between teacher and student

The transmission between a serious teacher and a serious student is largely pre-verbal. A summary written after a session survives; the session itself does not.

The machine can infer *about* that session, but it cannot be in the room. Much of what the room contains does not appear in the notes.

---

> These five places are the actual body of the contemplative work — and the machine cannot enter any of them.
>
> However rich this kind of system becomes, **the place of a teacher is not replaced**.
